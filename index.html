An explorable essay · the dials behind the headline

Two economies, one name

You have probably seen “the AI-agent economy” quoted as a single, confident number. Here is the quiet trouble with that number: there is no single economy behind it. There are two. One is a broad, loosely-defined software market; the other is a small, recently-cooled, crypto-native corner — and most coverage folds them into one tidy headline. This essay hands you the dials that headline was hiding. Move them yourself, and watch which economy you were actually being sold.

Try it: search “AI agent market size,” and a figure comes back stated with real confidence. Search once more and you’ll get another one, five or six times larger or smaller. The forecasters aren’t lying to you, and they aren’t quite disagreeing either — they’re quietly counting different things. Before we get to why that happens, let’s do the one thing every forecast does behind closed doors and never shows you: choose the assumptions for ourselves.

Every figure here is cited to a named public source — the research firms, on-chain APIs, and primary reports listed in Sources at the end. Where a default is uncertain, we say so on the figure itself. A recent snapshot renders first; live figures (where an API allows it) refresh on top, and a badge tells you which you’re seeing.

IIWhat’s actually inside it

“AI agents” was never one thing anyone measures — it’s a bundle of very different businesses wearing a single label. Drag the tiles to set the mix the way you picture it.

What “AI agents” actually means

Depending on where you draw the lines, that same headline figure tells four different stories. Algorithmic trading, call-centre automation, supply-chain software, and the small crypto-native sliver this essay is really chasing all ride under the same three words — and that crypto sliver is only a couple of percent of the whole. So the next time you see “the AI-agent market” sized in a headline, it’s worth asking one plain question: which of these did they actually have in mind? More often than not, that choice — not the technology — is what set the number.

IIIThe real engine

Set the token prices aside for a moment and look at the plumbing instead. The pattern worth watching is called x402, and the name comes from a long-forgotten corner of the web: when a server wants to be paid, it can answer your request with the status code “402 Payment Required.” For decades that code sat unused. x402 finally gives it a job — it lets one piece of software pay another for a single request, on the spot, with a tiny stablecoin payment and no account, invoice, or subscription in between.

IVPrice ≠ traction

One of the names here is TAO, the token of Bittensor — a network that pays its contributors for genuinely useful machine-learning work done by pieces of it called “subnets.” Flip the lens and the leaderboard rearranges itself in front of you. A framework like elizaOS all but disappears when you rank by token value (around $4M), then climbs back toward the top when you rank by real use (roughly 82,600 installs a month). The crypto-native corner did cool hard from its 2024–25 high — but the code never cooled with the prices. The two simply came apart. Anyone watching only the token charts missed the half of the story that kept quietly growing.

VWill enterprises actually adopt?

Adoption stories almost always get drawn as one smooth, reassuring S-curve. Set its ceiling, decide how steep it climbs, and pick the year it finally bends.

The adoption curve — and its attrition

—

The climb is real: enterprise use of agents rises from well under 1% in 2024 toward something like a third of companies by 2028. But that clean, confident line quietly hides the losses behind it. More than 40% of agent projects are expected to be cancelled outright, and where agents do reach production, they often run shallow — a pilot here, a single narrow task there. Drag the bend to an earlier year and the optimism looks irresistible. The honest part is the space the smooth line skips over: the long distance between adopting something and actually adopting it deeply.
